## 1. The Angle: "The one conversation you keep putting off"

**The Angle:** Every competitor markets around peace of mind, security, and legacy. They're talking about themselves. After Me's angle is different — it acknowledges the real human truth: you know you need to do this, you've been avoiding it, and here's why today is the day. The product's directness is its entire personality. Don't soften it in marketing the way competitors do. Lean in.

**Core Message:** "You've thought about it. You just haven't done anything about it. After Me takes 20 minutes. Your family will never be left searching."

This works because it doesn't lecture, it doesn't shame — it meets the user exactly where they are (avoidance) and removes the obstacle (perceived effort). The emotional truth is: people don't fear death as much as they fear leaving their family in chaos.

## The Four Emotional Triggers

These are the real reasons people buy, ordered by potency:

### Trigger 1 — "My family would be lost"
Not fear of death. Fear of leaving people you love without a map. This is the most potent. Use it first, always.

### Trigger 2 — "I don't trust the cloud"
Especially relevant post-40. People with real assets are paranoid about their sensitive data. "Nobody can access it but you" is a relief, not a feature.

### Trigger 3 — "Subscriptions are a scam"
Subscription fatigue is reshaping how people pay for software in 2025 — consumers actively seek ownership over recurring fees. "Pay once, yours forever" resonates deeply.

### Trigger 4 — "A life event just happened"
New child, parent diagnosis, friend's sudden death, turning 40/50. These are the purchase triggers. Marketing must intercept these moments.

## The 7 Selling Points

### SP1: Your documents never leave your phone
Don't say "local-first architecture" or "zero-knowledge encryption" — no one cares what it's called. Say: "Your will, your passport, your insurance — locked on your phone. We can't see it. Hackers can't reach it. It never touches a server." This is the hardest differentiator to copy and the one that most directly addresses the cloud-trust gap every competitor has.

### SP2: Your family can open it with a QR code — no app, no password, no account
This is the survivor flow distilled. It's operationally remarkable and nobody else offers it. The message: "After you're gone, your daughter just points her camera at a piece of paper. Everything opens. No passwords to guess, no accounts to recover, no lawyers to call first." This should be the first demo in every video ad and App Store preview.

### SP3: One payment. No subscription. Yours forever.
In 2025, winning products are the ones that match pricing to user expectations — and consumers want ownership over endless renewals. This isn't just a pricing decision, it's a values statement. "While every competitor charges you every month for the rest of your life, we think that's wrong. Pay once. It's yours." Lean into this as a moral stance, not just a deal.

### SP4: Even if After Me shuts down, your family can still open it
This is the open format guarantee. No competitor can say this. Position it as radical transparency: "We publish the file format. We publish the decoder. We believe your documents belong to you — not to us." This directly kills the #1 objection to any digital estate product.

### SP5: Takes 20 minutes. Done.
Procrastination is the real competitor. Emphasise radical simplicity. "Most people spend years meaning to sort this out. After Me takes one Sunday afternoon. Scan your documents. Print a piece of paper. Give it to someone you trust. Done." Concrete time commitment removes the "I'll do it later" response.

### SP6: Set it up for your parents too
The Family Pack angle. Your 40-year-old user is also worried about their 68-year-old mother who has never organised her documents. "Give your parents the gift of sorted. One family pack sets up everyone." This unlocks a gifting narrative and the family-tier pricing.

### SP7: A message in your own words, waiting for them
The Personal Messages feature — even in text-only v1 form — is emotionally the most powerful thing in the product. "Write a letter to your children. Record a voice message for your partner. After Me delivers them when the time comes." This is what makes people cry in the demo video. Use it.

## Channels — Where to Shout Each Selling Point

### App Store
The most important real estate you own. Screenshots should tell the story without reading:
- Screenshot 1: survivor scanning QR code, documents appearing (SP2)
- Screenshot 2: the "documents never leave your phone" privacy visual (SP1)
- Screenshot 3: "one payment" pricing screen (SP3)
- Screenshot 4: document categories filled with progress rings (SP5)

First line of description: "The one conversation you've been putting off. After Me takes 20 minutes." 80–90% of all trials happen on Day 0 — your onboarding experience is your one shot. The App Store page is the warm-up act for that shot.

### Short Video
Instagram Reels, TikTok, YouTube Shorts — 30–60 seconds. This is your primary paid acquisition channel for under-50s. The single most effective format: show the survivor flow. Open on a person receiving a small envelope in the post. Cut to them opening it — a printed card with a QR code. Text on screen: "My dad set this up before he died." Cut to phone camera scanning it. Documents appearing. Cut to black: "After Me. Everything your family needs." No voiceover. No product demo. Just the emotional moment.

### Facebook / Meta
Primary channel for 45–65 demographic. Your core demographic (45–65) is still predominantly on Facebook. Target: life event triggers (recently had a grandchild, recently bereaved, approaching retirement). Carousel ad format works well here — "5 documents your family would need right now if something happened to you." Each slide is a document type. Last slide: "After Me organises all of them. One-time purchase." Lead with fear of leaving family stranded (Trigger 1), close with "no subscription" relief (Trigger 3).

### Search / ASO
Intercept high-intent moments. People search for this after a life event. Target keywords: "digital will app", "store important documents iPhone", "family emergency documents", "estate planning app no subscription", "after death document organiser". The "no subscription" modifier is a high-converting long-tail keyword — consumers in 2025 are actively searching for ownership-based alternatives to subscription software.

### Content / SEO
Long-game organic — build the search moat. Create a free "Family Document Checklist" — a one-page PDF of the 15 documents every family needs to have accessible. Gate it with an email capture. This becomes your top-of-funnel lead magnet and ranks organically for "what documents does my family need if I die." Write 4–6 blog posts targeting: "what happens to your digital accounts when you die", "how to organise important documents for your family", "best apps for estate planning UK/US 2025".

### Partnerships
Distribution multipliers — highest ROI, often ignored:
- **Estate planning solicitors/attorneys**: offer a white-label or referral partnership. After clients sign a will, solicitors hand them an After Me flyer: "Now store it safely."
- **Financial advisors and IFAs**: same logic — they already have the trust relationship and the life event context
- **Life insurance companies**: After Me could be a bundled perk with new policies
- **Funeral planning companies**: people pre-planning funerals are exactly your demographic

These partnerships cost nothing to initiate and carry more credibility than any ad.

### PR
One story that writes itself. The pitch to journalists: "We built the only estate planning app that works even if we go out of business." That's a genuinely unusual tech company claim and it will get coverage in personal finance, tech, and lifestyle press. Target: The Times Money section, Which?, MoneySavingExpert (UK), Wirecutter, The Verge (US). The "open format guarantee" is the PR hook — it's contrarian in a world of cloud lock-in and genuinely newsworthy.

### Gifting
The overlooked channel — "give it to someone you love". After Me has a natural gifting dynamic: adult children setting it up for their parents, spouses gifting it to each other, financial advisors recommending it to clients. Create a gift-purchase flow: "Buy After Me for someone who matters." This turns every satisfied user into a distribution channel at Mother's Day, Father's Day, Christmas, and birthdays.

## Go-to-Market Timeline

### Pre-launch (now → launch)
- Build email waitlist with the "Family Document Checklist" lead magnet
- Get 5–10 beta users from target demographic
- Film the survivor flow demo video — this is your launch asset
- Reach out to 3 estate planning solicitors for partnership conversations

### Launch (weeks 1–4)
- App Store optimised with story-first screenshots
- Release the demo video as organic content across Instagram, TikTok, Facebook
- Submit to Product Hunt
- Email the personal finance press with the "open format" PR angle
- Launch Facebook carousel targeting life events

### Growth (months 2–6)
- Double down on whatever channel drove most installs
- Launch the gifting flow
- Start the partnership referral programme with solicitors/IFAs
- Publish 4 SEO blog posts
- A/B test $49.99 vs $79.99
- Launch YouTube Shorts with the survivor story format

## The One Thing That Will Make or Break All of This

**You need one real story.** Not a testimonial. Not a 5-star review. A real person, on camera, talking about the moment they used After Me after losing someone. "When my mum died, I found the printed QR code in her drawer. I scanned it. Everything was there." That 60-second video will outperform every piece of paid advertising you ever run.

Find that person before you spend a penny on ads. It doesn't have to be a real user — a staged version with an actor will work for launch — but make it feel real. Every great consumer app in this emotional category (life insurance, wills, organ donation) was built on a single story that made people feel something. Find yours.
